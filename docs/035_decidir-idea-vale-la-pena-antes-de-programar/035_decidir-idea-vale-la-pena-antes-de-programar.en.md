Having ideas is not the problem. The problem is opening the editor too soon.

Today, turning an idea into a first version is easier than ever. Claude Code, Cursor, Lovable, Replit, v0, n8n, and dozens of other tools can massively accelerate the build process. But that speed creates a trap: now you can lose a week building something that would have taken you a month before. That is better, yes. But it is still a lost week if the idea never had a user, a painful problem, demand, or a channel.

The important question is not "Can I build this?" With AI, the answer is often yes.

The question is: **does this idea deserve to be built now?**

This guide gives you a practical filter to decide before you start coding. It is written for technical founders, solopreneurs, and builders with an endless list of ideas in Notion, Apple Notes, or WhatsApp, but who need a colder way to choose what deserves to become code.

The central thesis is simple:

> An idea is worth building when you can describe exactly who needs it, what urgent pain it solves, how you will find the first users, and what minimum version you can deliver in one week.

If you cannot answer that, you do not have an idea ready to build. You have material to investigate.

---

## The mistake: confusing clarity with demand

A recent post in r/SaaS described a very common problem: the author had around 40 saved ideas and used to choose whichever one felt most exciting that day. The result: they shipped maybe 1 out of every 20.

To fix that, they started using three filters before opening Claude Code:

- Can I describe the user in one sentence without saying "people who..."?
- Can I describe the problem in one sentence without using "and"?
- Is there a small enough version to ship in one week?

They also put the idea into a PRD-generation tool to see whether the specification came out coherent. If the spec was vague, the idea was not formed yet.

That filter is good, but incomplete.

The community pointed out the key nuance: **clarity is not demand**.

The fact that you can explain an idea clearly does not mean anyone wants it. It also does not mean the problem is urgent, that someone will pay to solve it, or that you have a viable way to reach the first users.

An idea can look perfect in a specification and still be useless in the market.

That is why the right process has two layers:

1. **Internal clarity:** do you understand what you want to build, for whom, and with what scope?
2. **External signal:** are there real people who already feel this pain, search for solutions, or pay with time or money to solve it?

AI tools help with the first layer. They do not replace the second.

## The 7-step filter before coding

Use this process as a gate. It is not bureaucracy. It is a way to avoid building on impulse.

### 1. Write the user without hiding behind "people who"

A weak idea usually starts like this:

- "An app for people who want to be more productive."
- "A tool for businesses that use AI."
- "A dashboard for teams that need to stay better organized."

The problem with those sentences is that they sound specific, but they are not. "People who want to be more productive" could mean students, lawyers, founders, sales reps, freelancers, support teams, or content creators. Each group has different pains, budgets, channels, and urgency.

A better sentence identifies a concrete profile:

- "Owners of online academies who sell through WhatsApp and lose leads because they reply too late."
- "Small agencies that handle monthly reporting in spreadsheets for 10-30 clients."
- "B2B consultants who take discovery calls but do not turn notes into proposals fast enough."

The practical rule:

> If you cannot name 3-5 real people who match the user, the user is probably still imaginary.

They do not need to be confirmed customers. But they do need to be concrete humans: someone you know, someone you spoke with, someone you saw complaining in a community, someone who published a workaround.

If you can only describe an abstract "ideal persona," you are still in strategic fantasy.

### 2. Define the problem in one sentence without bundling three problems together

Another sign of an immature idea is the compound problem:

- "Helps entrepreneurs organize tasks, automate follow-up, and improve sales."
- "Lets restaurants manage reservations, promotions, and customer service."
- "Automates content, analytics, and distribution for creators."

That is not a problem. It is a wishlist.

To validate, you need one sentence with one main pain:

- "They lose leads because they answer WhatsApp inquiries several hours late."
- "They spend 6 hours per month copying campaign metrics into a manual report."
- "They forget to follow up after sales calls and lose warm opportunities."

A good problem sentence has three characteristics:

- **It is observable:** you can see the behavior or the consequence.
- **It has a cost:** time, money, risk, reputation, or anxiety.
- **It does not depend on your solution:** the problem exists even if your product does not.

If you need to explain your solution before the problem sounds important, you probably do not understand the pain yet.

### 3. Ask "why now?"

An idea can be good and still not be urgent.

The question "why now?" separates latent problems from active problems. It is not enough for someone to have a pain. You need to understand why that pain became important enough today.

Good answers:

- "They are receiving more leads than they can answer manually."
- "Acquisition cost went up, so they can no longer afford to lose opportunities."
- "They hired a team and the informal process started breaking."
- "A platform, regulatory, or market change made the old method stop working."
- "AI lowered the technical cost, and now something that used to be expensive can be done in days."

Bad answers:

- "Because AI is popular."
- "Because it would be useful."
- "Because nobody has done it well."
- "Because I would use it someday."

"Someday" kills products. Good early ideas usually live close to pains that are already creating friction this week.

### 4. Look for evidence that they already pay with time, money, or embarrassment

Do not start by asking "would you pay for this?" People often answer politely, optimistically, or hypothetically.

Watch what they do today.

Strong demand signals:

- They pay for an imperfect tool.
- They hire someone to do it manually.
- They use spreadsheets, Zapier, n8n, Airtable, or fragile scripts to solve it.
- They ask in communities, "Is there a tool that does X?"
- They share repeated complaints using the same language.
- They have a painful manual process, but keep doing it because the result matters.

Weak signals:

- They say "interesting."
- They like a post.
- They join a waitlist with no context.
- Friends say the idea sounds good.
- An AI model generates a polished PRD.

A useful rule:

> If nobody is paying to solve the problem, at least look for evidence that they are already paying with time, effort, or risk.

In small businesses, this often appears as patchwork solutions: huge Google Sheets, WhatsApp voice notes, virtual assistants, hand-connected forms, monthly reports copied from five platforms, and so on.

That is where opportunity often lives, because you are not inventing the pain. You are replacing a workaround.

### 5. Find the channel before building

Many builders validate the product, but not the distribution.

Before coding, answer:

- Where do these people live?
- What communities do they read?
- What do they search for on Google or YouTube?
- Who do they follow?
- What words do they use to describe the problem?
- How would I get the first 10 users if I had to do it this week?

If you cannot find 10 potential users, you are not ready to build. You are ready to research.

This does not mean you need a full growth strategy. You only need a credible initial route.

Examples:

- "I will contact 20 academy owners who already sell through WhatsApp and offer to review their lead flow."
- "I will publish a checklist in an agency group and ask for examples of manual reporting."
- "I will search Reddit, LinkedIn, and Slack groups for phrases like 'manual reporting,' 'WhatsApp leads,' and 'forgot to follow up,' then collect real complaints."

Early distribution starts as manual work. If you do not know where to do that manual work, building more features will not fix it.

### 6. Reduce scope to a one-week version

The "ship it in one week" rule is brutally useful because it forces you to cut fantasy.

Do not ask: "What is the ideal product?"

Ask:

> What is the smallest version that delivers a real result to a real person in 7 days?

Examples:

- Do not build an "AI CRM for WhatsApp." Build "automatic WhatsApp lead capture into a spreadsheet with follow-up reminders."
- Do not build a "reporting platform for agencies." Build "a monthly report generator from one template and 3 key metrics."
- Do not build a "complete assistant for sales proposals." Build "turn discovery notes into a first proposal draft."

The one-week version does not have to scale. It has to confirm whether the pain exists and whether the result matters.

YC often insists on talking to users, launching fast, and using the MVP as a learning process, not as the final product. That idea is especially important now: with AI, you can build fast, but the goal of the MVP is not to prove that you can build. It is to discover whether someone wants the result.

### 7. Use AI to pressure-test the idea, not to fool yourself

An AI tool can help you detect vagueness:

- Ask it to write the target user.
- Ask it to define the problem in one sentence.
- Ask it to propose a one-week MVP.
- Ask it to identify unvalidated assumptions.
- Ask it to generate interview questions.

But be careful: models are very good at turning vague ideas into convincing documents.

A polished PRD can make an idea feel more mature than it is. The test is not whether AI can write a specification. The test is whether that specification connects to real evidence.

Use this prompt as a filter:

```text
Act as a brutal SaaS idea evaluator.

Idea: [describe the idea]
Target user: [one sentence]
Problem: [one sentence]
Observed evidence: [complaints, workarounds, payments, interviews]
Initial channel: [where I will find the first 10 users]
7-day MVP: [what I will build]

Evaluate:
1. What part is clear.
2. What part is an assumption.
3. What evidence is missing before coding.
4. What smaller version could be tested this week.
5. Signals that would mean I should kill the idea.

Do not be nice. Look for weaknesses.
```

If the model responds with too much confidence but you do not have external evidence, do not move forward. Go back to talking to users.

## Scorecard: decide whether to build, research, or kill

Before opening the editor, score the idea from 0 to 2 on each dimension.

### 1. Specific user

- **0:** "people who..." or a segment that is too broad.
- **1:** clear segment, but no real people identified.
- **2:** clear segment plus 3-5 real people or concrete communities.

### 2. Observable pain

- **0:** generic or aspirational benefit.
- **1:** clear problem, but no visible cost.
- **2:** problem with an obvious cost in time, money, risk, or stress.

### 3. Urgency / "why now"

- **0:** would be useful someday.
- **1:** there is interest, but no urgency.
- **2:** there is current pressure to solve it.

### 4. Behavioral evidence

- **0:** only opinions or enthusiasm.
- **1:** some complaints or weak signals.
- **2:** workarounds, payments, active searches, or repeated manual processes.

### 5. Initial channel

- **0:** you do not know where to find users.
- **1:** you have a channel hypothesis.
- **2:** you know exactly where to contact or observe the first 10 users.

### 6. One-week MVP

- **0:** requires building a full platform.
- **1:** can be reduced, but is still too big.
- **2:** there is a functional test deliverable in 7 days.

### 7. Controlled technical risk

- **0:** the hardest part is uncertain technology.
- **1:** there is one technically uncertain piece.
- **2:** the technology is viable; the main risk is demand.

Interpretation:

- **0-6 points:** do not code. Reformulate or kill the idea.
- **7-10 points:** investigate more. Talk to users, look for workarounds, reduce scope.
- **11-14 points:** build a one-week test.

The goal is not to get a perfect score. The goal is to prevent an idea with 2 points of excitement and 0 points of demand from reaching your development calendar.

## The best questions to validate without bias

Do not ask:

- "Would you like an app that does X?"
- "Would you pay for this?"
- "Do you think this is a good idea?"
- "Would you use an AI tool to solve this?"

Those questions invite polite answers.

Ask about past and present behavior:

- "When was the last time this happened to you?"
- "What did you do to solve it?"
- "How long did it take?"
- "What happens if you do not solve it?"
- "What tools do you use today?"
- "Do you pay for any related solution?"
- "Who else is involved in this process?"
- "Which part annoys you the most?"
- "If this disappeared tomorrow, what would change in your week?"

The Mom Test points in this direction: talk about the user's life, not your idea. Validation improves when you stop asking for approval and start investigating facts.

A good interview does not end with "they liked my idea." It ends with specific phrases, examples, and behaviors that help you decide what to do.

## Example: applying the filter to an AI automation idea

Initial idea:

> "An AI agent for businesses that answer WhatsApp."

It sounds good, but it is too broad.

After the filter:

**User:** owners of small online academies that sell courses through WhatsApp and receive 20-100 inquiries per week.

**Problem:** they lose sales because they reply late, forget to follow up, and do not know which leads are warm.

**Why now:** they are investing more in content and ads, but message volume has exceeded their manual capacity.

**Evidence:** they use manual WhatsApp labels, spreadsheets, part-time assistants, and internal voice notes to remember follow-ups.

**Initial channel:** educational entrepreneur groups, Instagram and YouTube accounts from academies, direct contacts with creators who sell programs.

**One-week MVP:** export or capture inbound leads, classify them by intent, and create follow-up reminders in a spreadsheet or simple CRM. No full chatbot.

This version can still die. But now it is an idea you can investigate and test without building a huge platform.

## Signs you should kill the idea

Killing an idea is not failure. It is protecting your focus.

Kill it or put it on hold if:

- You cannot name real users.
- The problem only appears when you explain it.
- Nobody has a workaround.
- Nobody has searched for a solution.
- The user has no budget or authority.
- You cannot reach the first users.
- The minimum MVP is still too large.
- The idea excites you more because of the technology than because of the pain.
- All positive responses come from friends.

The most dangerous signal is this: **you struggle to abandon the idea because you have already imagined the full product**.

When that happens, you are validating your identity as a builder, not a market opportunity.

---

## When you should start building

Start building when you have a sufficient combination of these signals:

- Concrete user.
- Clear and costly problem.
- Current urgency.
- Real people identified.
- Evidence of workarounds or spending.
- Credible initial channel.
- MVP deliverable in one week.
- Explicit hypothesis about what you will learn from the first version.

The first version should have a learning question:

- "Will users accept connecting their WhatsApp, or do they prefer uploading exports?"
- "Does the automatic summary actually save time?"
- "Would they pay to receive the finished report, or do they only want the template?"
- "Is the pain in capturing leads or in following up?"

If you do not know what question your MVP answers, you are probably building out of anxiety.

## Final checklist before opening Claude Code

Copy this and use it every time an idea tempts you.

```text
Idea:

User in one sentence:

Problem in one sentence:

Why now:

3-5 real people or communities where this user appears:

Evidence of current pain:

Workarounds or current solutions:

What they are paying today with time/money/risk:

Where I will find the first 10 users:

7-day MVP:

What I am NOT going to build yet:

Main question the MVP must answer:

Signal to continue:

Signal to kill the idea:
```

If you cannot complete most fields, you are not ready to code. You are ready to investigate.

## Conclusion: faster building requires more discipline, not less

AI reduced the cost of building. But it did not reduce the cost of choosing badly.

In fact, it made that more dangerous: when coding becomes easy, the temptation to build half-formed ideas increases. You can produce prototypes, landing pages, and PRDs at absurd speed. But if there is no user, pain, channel, and demand, you are only manufacturing fake evidence of progress.

The modern builder's discipline is not writing more code. It is knowing when not to write it yet.

Before opening Claude Code, pass the idea through this filter:

- specific user,
- simple problem,
- real urgency,
- external evidence,
- initial channel,
- one-week MVP.

If it passes, build small and learn fast.

If it does not, do not call it failure. Call it good judgment.
